LEGAL BASIS

17. Section 39 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872 provides that when a party to a contract has refused to perform, or disabled himself from performing, his promise in its entirety, the promisee may put an end to the contract. In the present case, the Defendant's unilateral termination without compliance with the notice clause constitutes a refusal to perform its obligations under the agreement, entitling the Plaintiff to seek damages for breach.

18. Section 73 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872 provides that when a contract has been broken, the party who suffers by such breach is entitled to receive, as compensation for any loss or damage caused to him thereby, compensation for any loss or damage caused to him which naturally arose in the usual course of things from such breach, or which the parties knew, when they made the contract, to be likely to result from the breach of it. The Plaintiff's losses — being loss of profit, goodwill, wasted investment, and unsold stock — are all losses that naturally arose from the Defendant's breach and were within the contemplation of the parties at the time of entering into the dealership agreement.

19. In the alternative, without prejudice to the primary claim, the Plaintiff invokes Section 74 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872, to the extent that any liquidated damages clause in the agreement may be applicable, subject to the Court's assessment of reasonable compensation.
