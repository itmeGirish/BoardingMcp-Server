FACTS OF THE CASE

1. The Plaintiff and the Defendants are Hindu co-owners of an ancestral joint family property, having inherited the same from their deceased father, on whose death the Hindu succession rules of coparcenary applied.

2. The property comprises two schedules: (a) Residential house No. 42, 3rd Cross, Jayanagar, Bangalore measuring 2400 sq.ft.; and (b) Agricultural land, Survey No. 85, Anekal Taluk, Bangalore Rural measuring 2 acres.

3. The property is held as ancestral joint family property, with rights of coparcenary in the male line, and the Plaintiff claims a 1/3rd share as a co-owner.

4. The Plaintiff has repeatedly requested the Defendants to partition the joint family property and to grant the Plaintiff his rightful share, but the Defendants have denied the Plaintiffs entitlement and refused to effect any partition.

5. The Plaintiff has remained in possession of his share only as a co-owner and continues to be excluded from exclusive possession of any specific portion until partition is effected.

6. To enforce the Plaintiffs rights and to avoid further dispute, the Plaintiff seeks partition by metes and bounds and separate possession of his 1/3rd share, along with appointment of a Commissioner to make the partition.

7. The Plaintiff also seeks mesne profits for the period during which the Plaintiff has been excluded from possession due to the Defendants refusal to partition and hand over possession.

LEGAL BASIS

12. Order XX Rule 18 CPC enables the Court to order partition of immovable property held in common and to appoint a Commissioner to make such partition and report back to the Court. The present suit is for partition and separate possession; hence Order XX Rule 18 CPC provides a direct procedural basis for appointment of a Commissioner to effect the partition.

13. Hindu Succession Act, 1956: Sections 6, 8 and 10 recognise coparcenary rights and devolution of interest in joint family property, thereby underpinning the Plaintiffs entitlement to a share and the right to seek partition and separate possession.

14. Partition Act, 1893: Sections 2, 3 and 4 empower Courts to determine and declare rights in joint family properties and to effect partitions by metes and bounds, including appointment of Commissioners where necessary.

15. Indian Succession Act, 1925: Sections 211 and 218 relate to succession and the distribution of estate of a deceased person, supporting the acquisition of rights by the Plaintiff as a coparcener and heir and the entitlement to pursue partition and possession.

16. The Defendantsefensive posture that there is no prior partition is anticipated; however, no partition has taken place, and the Plaintiff remains a co-owner with a right to enforce partition and possession under the cited statutes.

17. The Plaintiff seeks appointment of a Commissioner under Order XX Rule 18 CPC to demarcate and deliver the Plaintiffs 1/3rd share by metes and bounds and to report to the Court for final decree.

CAUSE OF ACTION

Origin and accrual: The Plaintiff's cause of action arises on the death of the father, when coparcenary rights in the ancestral joint family property attached to the Plaintiff as a Hindu coparcener and co-owner. The Defendants' ongoing denial of partition and possession constitutes a continuing cause of action, and the Plaintiff's rights remain unsettled so long as partition remains undetermined.
