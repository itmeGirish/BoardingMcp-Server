SUIT FOR DAMAGES FOR ILLEGAL TERMINATION OF dealership/AGENCY/FRANCHISE AGREEMENT AND RELATED RELIEFS UNDER SECTIONS 39 AND 73 OF THE INDIAN CONTRACT ACT, 1872

MOST RESPECTFULLY SHOWETH:

JURISDICTION

1. The Plaintiff is a person sui juris and competent to institute the present suit. The Defendant is likewise a person sui juris and competent to be sued.

2. This Hon'ble Court has territorial and pecuniary jurisdiction to try and entertain the present suit as the dealership agreement was to operate within the territory of {{TERRITORY_PLACE}} under the jurisdiction of this Court, the breach arose within such territorial limits, and the value of the claim exceeds the lower pecuniary limits of this Court.

3. This Hon'ble Court has subject matter jurisdiction to entertain damages claims arising from breach of an exclusive dealership/agency/franchise agreement of this nature under the Indian Contract Act, 1872.

SECTION 12A COMPLIANCE

4. The Plaintiff states that in compliance with Section 12A of the Commercial Courts Act, 2015, pre-institution mediation was attempted through {{MEDIATION_AUTHORITY}} but was unsuccessful / the Defendant failed to participate.

FACTS OF THE CASE

5. The Plaintiff invested Rs. 50,00,000/- (Rupees Fifty Lakh) as capital in establishing and developing the dealership in the Territory and, over a period of five years, built a substantial market presence including customer base, inventory, supply chains, and brand presence, as evidenced by Annexures A and B.

6. The Defendant entered into a dealership agreement with the Plaintiff conferring exclusive rights in the Territory, with terms including a mandatory six-month prior termination notice as per Clause {{TERMINATION_NOTICE_CLAUSE}} of the agreement (true copy annexed as Annexure A).

7. On or about {{TERMINATION_DATE}}, the Defendant terminated the dealership agreement by serving a notice of only 15 days, which is a clear violation of the six-month termination notice clause and is arbitrary and unjustified (termination letter attached as Annexure D).

8. The termination was not only non-compliant with the express termination notice requirement but also devoid of any valid business justification and due process. It was a unilateral act by the Defendant in breach of the express terms of the agreement.

9. As a result of the termination, the Plaintiff has incurred and continues to incur damages, including: (i) loss of profit for the unexpired term of the dealership amounting to Rs. 25,00,000/-; (ii) loss of goodwill in the Territory valued at Rs. 15,00,000/-; (iii) unsold stock valued at Rs. 10,00,000/-; (iv) capital invested of Rs. 50,00,000/- which is now stranded/wasted due to abrupt termination; the details and supporting documents are referenced in Annexures B and C.

10. The Plaintiff has at every stage attempted to resolve the dispute amicably by correspondence and negotiations. Copies of the reminders/communications are annexed as Annexure C.

11. The Plaintiff served a legal notice dated {{NOTICE_DATE}} through registered post A.D. calling upon the Defendant to remedy the breach and compensate the Plaintiff within the stipulated period; a copy of the notice and postal receipts is annexed as Annexure E. The Defendant’s non-response or evasive response further evidences the breach.

12. The breach of contract by the Defendant has caused, and will continue to cause, substantial loss and damages to the Plaintiff, who seeks remedy in this Court under the appropriate provisions of law.

LEGAL BASIS

13. The Plaintiff submits that the Defendant is liable under:
   (a) Section 39 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872: Anticipatory breach/repudiation of contract, wherein if a party repudiates the contract before the due date, the other party may sue for damages for loss arising naturally from such breach. In the present case, the termination of the dealership agreement by the Defendant constitutes repudiation and breach on the date of termination, causing the Plaintiff to suffer the losses pleaded herein.
   (b) Section 73 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872: Damages for breach of contract, which provides that damages should be such as naturally arise in the usual course of things from such breach, or which the parties would reasonably have contemplated at the time of contract as the probable result of the breach.
   (c) Without prejudice to the above, Section 74 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872 may be invoked in the alternative for any penalty/damages where applicable, subject to the Court’s discretion and the facts of the case.

CAUSE OF ACTION

14. The cause of action arose on the date of termination, i.e., {{TERMINATION_DATE}}, when the Defendant terminated the dealership agreement by serving a notice of 15 days, contrary to the six-month notice clause in the agreement.

15. The cause of action further arose on the date of service/non-reply to the protest/notice dated {{NOTICE_DATE}}; the Plaintiff's right to damages remained, and the Defendant has continued to benefit from the abrupt termination by retaining/utilising the Plaintiff’s investment and market development in the Territory.

16. The cause of action is a SINGLE EVENT breach (illegal termination) with damages arising therefrom, and the Plaintiff is not seeking to plead a continuing breach, though the consequences and losses continue to subsist.

LIMITATION

17. The suit is within the limitation period prescribed under Article 55 of the Schedule to the Limitation Act, 1963, the accrual being the date of illegal termination, i.e., {{TERMINATION_DATE}}, and the suit is filed within three years from that date.
