{{PLAINTIFF_NAME}} v. {{DEFENDANT_NAME}}

FACTS OF THE CASE

1. The Plaintiff is a dealer who operated under a dealership arrangement with the Defendant for development of the designated territory.

2. The Plaintiff invested Rs.50,00,000/- as capital to establish and develop the territory and has actively built the market over a period of five years.

3. On {{TERMINATION_DATE}}, the Defendant terminated the dealership with only 15 days' notice, thereby breaching the termination clause and the express terms of the agreement.

4. The Plaintiff promptly protested the abrupt termination and asserted that such termination was arbitrary and not in accordance with the contract terms.

5. The Plaintiff fulfilled all material performance obligations under the dealership agreement and conducted business in accordance with the contract until termination.

6. Over the five-year period the Plaintiff invested in market development, branding, inventory, manpower and promotional activities within the territory.

7. As a result of the termination, the Plaintiff has suffered loss of profits for the unexpired term quantified at Rs.25,00,000/-.

8. The abrupt termination has caused loss of goodwill in the market quantified at Rs.15,00,000/-.

9. There remains unsold stock valued at Rs.10,00,000/-.

10. The total capital investment of Rs.50,00,000/- stands squandered due to termination and the Plaintiff seeks compensation for the same.

11. The Plaintiff served a legal notice dated {{NOTICE_DATE}} demanding restoration of rights or compensation; the Defendant failed to rectify the breach or respond satisfactorily.

12. The Plaintiff seeks damages for breach of contract in a Commercial Court on the basis that the termination was illegal, in breach of the express terms of the dealership agreement, and caused substantial loss to the Plaintiff's business interests.

LEGAL BASIS

13. Indian Contract Act, 1872 — {{PROVISION_39}} provides that where a contract is broken, the promisee is entitled to receive compensation for any loss or damage caused thereby that naturally arose in the usual course of things from such breach.

14. Indian Contract Act, 1872 — {{PROVISION_73}} provides for damages for breach of contract, i.e., compensation for loss of injury caused by the breach, including loss of profit where such profit would naturally have occurred in the ordinary course of things.

15. Indian Contract Act, 1872 — {{PROVISION_55}} deals with terms relating to contingent contracts and conditions; used here to assert that termination in breach of contractual notice clauses is not excused by any implied term.

16. Alternative pleading: Indian Contract Act, 1872 — {{PROVISION_74}} (without prejudice): in appropriate circumstances, damages may be claimed under other provisions for breach or misrepresentation.

17. Specific Relief Act, 1963 — {{PROVISION_SR_14}} (without prejudice): in the event monetary relief is inadequate or to tailor relief, the Court may grant appropriate relief to protect the Plaintiff's rights.

18. Limitation: Article 55, Limitation Act, 1963 — period of three years for compensation for breach of any contract, express or implied; accrual on the date of illegal termination.

CAUSE OF ACTION

19. The cause of action arose on {{TERMINATION_DATE}} when the Defendant terminated the dealership agreement in breach of the six months' notice clause.

20. The cause of action further arose on {{NOTICE_DATE}} upon the Defendant's failure to respond to the protest/legal notice.

21. The cause of action is not continuing — the breach occurred on a single date, but the consequences and losses flowing from the termination continue to affect the Plaintiff's business and rights, giving rise to the Plaintiff's entitlement to damages.

LIMITATION

Limitation: Article 55, Limitation Act, 1963: Period three years; accrual on termination date.
