FACTS OF THE CASE

5. The Plaintiff is engaged in the business of {{PLAINtIFF_BUSINESS_DESCRIPTION}} and, to expand operations, entered into a dealership agreement dated {{AGREEMENT_DATE}} with the Defendant for the territory of {{TERRITORY}} (a true copy of the dealership agreement is annexed hereto and marked as Annexure A).

6. Under the said dealership agreement, the Defendant undertook to appoint the Plaintiff as dealer for the specified territory and to provide support and brand access; in consideration, the Plaintiff undertook to invest capital, stock, and meet sales targets as detailed in the agreement.

7. In pursuance of the said agreement, the Plaintiff has invested substantial capital in establishing infrastructure, inventory, branding, and working capital, amounting to Rs. {{INVESTED_CAPITAL}} (proof of investments, invoices, and bank statements are annexed hereto and marked as Annexure B).

8. The Plaintiff actively developed the territory market, built a customer base, trained personnel, and achieved expected sales and profits as per the business plan and the agreement.

9. On {{TERMINATION_DATE}}, the Defendant terminated the dealership agreement (termination letter dated {{TERMINATION_LETTER_DATE}}; a true copy of which is annexed as Annexure C) without giving the notice, cure period, or following the procedure mandated by the agreement, and contrary to the express terms of the contract.

10. The termination was arbitrary and amounted to repudiation of the contract in violation of Section 39 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872, which provides that a party to a reciprocal contract cannot repudiate before performance; by terminating abruptly, the Defendant breached the contract and deprived the Plaintiff of expected profits and market opportunities.

11. The Plaintiff protested the termination and requested cure/remedy through a formal notice dated {{NOTICE_DATE}} (a copy annexed as Annexure D); no adequate explanation or remedy was offered, and the Defendant failed to honour the agreement or to provide adequate remedy.

12. The Plaintiff thereby suffered loss including: (i) loss of projected profits from the unexpired term, (ii) loss of goodwill and brand value generated in the territory, (iii) wastage of invested capital due to early termination, (iv) unsold stock as on the date of termination remaining in the Plaintiff's possession, and (v) potential future business opportunities foregone due to disruption of the dealership arrangement.

13. The Plaintiff notified the Defendant of the damages and demanded settlement; however, the Defendant did not respond or responded inadequately; consequently, the Plaintiff filed the present suit to seek redress.

LEGAL BASIS

14. Section 39 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872 provides that when a contract containing reciprocal promises is broken by one party, the other party is entitled to compensation for loss or damages caused by such breach. The termination of the dealership agreement constitutes repudiation of the contract before performance and thus triggers liability for damages; the Plaintiff seeks compensation for loss arising naturally from such breach, including profits that would have accrued during the unexpired term.

15. Section 73 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872 provides that when a contract is broken, the party suffering the breach is entitled to damages that naturally arise from such breach, subject to the principle of reasonable foreseeability; the Plaintiff's claims for loss of profit, goodwill, and unsold stock are within the scope of these damages.

16. In the alternative, without prejudice, Section 74 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872 provides that where a contract has been repudiated, the damages may be recovered under the provision for compensation for breach of contract; the Plaintiff's claim for loss of investment and goodwill is supported by this provision.

CAUSE OF ACTION

17. The cause of action for this suit arises on the date of termination, i.e., on {{TERMINATION_DATE}}, when the Defendant terminated the dealership agreement; the termination was a single-event breach, and the Plaintiff has suffered damages as a result.

18. 19. The Plaintiff’s damages are to be determined by the Court, and the Plaintiff seeks relief for loss of profit for the unexpired term, loss of goodwill, wasted capital investment, unsold stock, and future business opportunities.
